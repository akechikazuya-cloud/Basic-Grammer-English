===================================
AUXILIARY VERBS: "BE," "DO," "HAVE"
===================================

WHAT IS AN AUXILIARY VERB?
--------------------------------
An auxiliary verb is a "helper verb" — it teams up with a main verb 
to build different tenses, questions, negatives, or passive 
sentences. Think of it like a sidekick that supports the main verb so 
it can do more complex jobs.

Common auxiliary verbs include: be, do, have, will, shall, would, 
should, can, could, may, might, must, ought

  I might visit my cousins this weekend.
  She is baking a cake right now.
  We have finished the report.
  This letter was written by my grandmother.
  You should rest more.

Notice how each helper verb (might, is, have, was, should) supports 
the main verb next to it (visit, baking, finished, written, rest).


THE TRICKY PART: "BE," "DO," AND "HAVE" HAVE DOUBLE JOBS
------------------------------------------------------------------
Unlike "will" or "should," which are ALWAYS helper verbs, the words 
"be," "do," and "have" can work in two different ways:

  1. As a MAIN VERB (carrying the full meaning on its own)
  2. As an AUXILIARY VERB (just helping another verb)

Let's look at each one closely.


PART 1: "BE" AS AN AUXILIARY VERB
===================================

USE A: BUILDING PROGRESSIVE (CONTINUOUS) SENTENCES
------------------------------------------------------------
"Be" pairs with a verb + "-ing" to show an action in progress.

  I am painting the fence today.
  He is cooking dinner right now.
  They have been rehearsing for the play all week.
  We were watching a documentary when you called.

Here, "be" (am, is, have been, were) is just the helper — the real 
action is painting, cooking, rehearsing, watching.


USE B: BUILDING PASSIVE SENTENCES
--------------------------------------
"Be" also pairs with a past participle to build passive voice, where 
the subject receives the action instead of doing it.

  I was awarded a scholarship.
  She was recognized by her old classmates.
  This mural has been painted by a local artist.
  The package was delivered this morning.

Memory trick: Whenever you see "be" + "-ing," think progressive 
(action happening). Whenever you see "be" + past participle, think 
passive (action received).


PART 2: "DO" AS AN AUXILIARY VERB
===================================

USE A: BUILDING NEGATIVE SENTENCES
--------------------------------------
"Do/does/did" teams up with "not" to make a sentence negative.

  I do not understand this instruction.
  She doesn't like spicy food.
  They didn't finish the race.
  We don't have any plans this weekend.


USE B: BUILDING QUESTIONS
------------------------------
"Do/does/did" jumps to the front of the sentence to form a question.

  Do you want some tea?
  Does she live nearby?
  Did they call you back?
  Do we need to bring anything?

Memory trick: "Do" is like a flexible assistant — it steps in only 
when needed (for negatives and questions), then steps back when the 
sentence is simply a positive statement.


PART 3: "HAVE" AS AN AUXILIARY VERB
===================================

USE: BUILDING PERFECT TENSE SENTENCES
--------------------------------------------
"Have/has/had" pairs with a past participle to build perfect tense 
sentences, connecting one moment in time to another.

  I have been waiting here for twenty minutes.
  We have completed the entire project.
  She had been the manager of that store for years.
  They have already left the building.

Memory trick: "Have" acts like a bridge builder — connecting a past 
action to now, to another past moment, or eventually to the future 
(with "will have").

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try building one sentence for each use above, using your own daily 
life:

  BE (progressive): I am ____ right now.
  BE (passive): This ____ was made by ____.
  DO (negative): I do not ____.
  DO (question): Do you ____?
  HAVE (perfect): I have already ____ today.

The more you practice spotting these helper verbs in action, the 
easier it becomes to build accurate sentences in any tense or voice.
