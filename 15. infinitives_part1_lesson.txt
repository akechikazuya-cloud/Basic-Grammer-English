WHAT IS AN INFINITIVE?
--------------------------
An infinitive is simply the word "to" plus the base form of a verb 
(to + verb). Most of the time, an infinitive works like a noun in the 
sentence. Sometimes, though, it can work like an adjective or an 
adverb instead.

Formula: TO + BASE VERB = INFINITIVE (to go, to eat, to study, to win)


RULE 1: INFINITIVES OFTEN ACT AS THE OBJECT OF A VERB
------------------------------------------------------------
Many verbs are naturally followed by an infinitive.

  I want to travel next summer.
  She hopes to graduate this year.
  We plan to visit our grandparents.
  He decided to change his career.
  They promised to help us move.
  Do you need to leave early?


RULE 2: AN INFINITIVE CAN BE THE SUBJECT OF A SENTENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------
An infinitive can also start a sentence and act as the main subject — 
just like a gerund can.

  To travel the world is my biggest dream.
  To forgive someone is not always easy.
  To skip breakfast is a bad habit.

Memory trick: Both gerunds ("traveling") and infinitives ("to 
travel") can act like nouns and be the subject — English simply gives 
you two tools for the same job!


RULE 3: INFINITIVES CAN SHOW PURPOSE (WHY SOMEONE DID SOMETHING)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
When an infinitive answers the question "why?", it's acting like an 
adverb.

  She woke up early to catch the sunrise.
  I called him to apologize.
  We moved closer to hear the music better.
  He studies every night to improve his grades.

Memory trick: If you can replace the infinitive with "in order to," 
it's showing purpose.
  She woke up early in order to catch the sunrise.


RULE 4: INFINITIVES CAN DESCRIBE A NOUN (LIKE AN ADJECTIVE)
------------------------------------------------------------------
Sometimes an infinitive follows a noun and gives more detail about it, 
acting like an adjective.

  Here is our shopping list.
  I have a decision to make.
  This is the perfect time to start.
  She has a story to tell.


RULE 5: COMMON PATTERNS WITH INFINITIVES
------------------------------------------------
There are several everyday patterns that use infinitives. Here are a 
few of the most useful ones:

  It + be + adjective + infinitive:
    It was wonderful to see you again.
    It is important to stay hydrated.

  Adjective + enough + infinitive:
    I am old enough to make my own decisions.
    She is strong enough to lift that box.

  Verb + object + infinitive:
    Can you help me to fix this?
    The teacher asked us to be quiet.
    I want you to call me tonight.


RULE 6: THE NEGATIVE FORM PLACES "NOT" BEFORE "TO"
--------------------------------------------------------
To make an infinitive negative, simply put "not" right before "to."

  Remember not to be late tomorrow.
  She decided not to attend the party.
  Try not to worry so much.
  He promised not to interrupt again.


RULE 7: SPLIT INFINITIVES (USED FOR EMPHASIS)
--------------------------------------------------
Normally, we don't separate "to" from the verb. But sometimes, for 
strong emphasis, a word is placed between them. This is called a 
"split infinitive."

  I need you to really focus on this.
  You have to truly believe in yourself.
  She wants to finally finish the marathon.

Memory trick: Splitting the infinitive is like sliding an extra 
spotlight word right in the middle, to shine extra attention on the 
verb. It's grammatically acceptable but should be used sparingly, 
mainly for emphasis.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Practice building sentences using each role of the infinitive:

  I want to ____. (object of a verb)
  To ____ is important to me. (subject)
  I exercise to ____. (purpose)
  I have a ____ to ____. (describing a noun)

Try filling in your own words to see how flexible infinitives can be!
