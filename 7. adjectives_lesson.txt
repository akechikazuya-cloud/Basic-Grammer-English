WHAT IS AN ADJECTIVE?
------------------------
An adjective describes or gives more detail about a noun. It answers 
questions like: What kind? How many? Which one?

Read this short passage:
  I love rainy days. A rainy day is a quiet, cozy time with soft gray 
  skies and the gentle sound of raindrops. It makes me feel calm and 
  helps my busy mind slow down.

Notice the describing words: rainy, quiet, cozy, soft, gray, gentle, 
calm, busy. Each one adds color and detail to a noun.


RULE 1: ADJECTIVES USUALLY COME BEFORE THE NOUN
----------------------------------------------------
In English, the adjective almost always sits right in front of the 
noun it describes.

  A funny joke
  Blue skies
  A short pencil
  Heavy suitcases
  Cold water

Memory trick: Think of the adjective as a "label" you stick on the 
noun right before you say it — like a sticky note on a box.


RULE 2: MAKING OPPOSITES WITH PREFIXES
-------------------------------------------
Many adjectives can flip to their opposite meaning just by adding a 
small prefix at the front: un-, in-, or dis-.

  UN-:
    happy --> unhappy
    fair --> unfair
    kind --> unkind
    limited --> unlimited
    healthy --> unhealthy
    interesting --> uninteresting
    comfortable --> uncomfortable
    usual --> unusual

  IN-:
    accurate --> inaccurate
    formal --> informal
    visible --> invisible
    active --> inactive
    direct --> indirect
    tolerant --> intolerant

  DIS-:
    honest --> dishonest
    obedient --> disobedient
    loyal --> disloyal
    connect --> disconnect

Tip: There's no perfect rule for which prefix goes with which word — 
this comes with practice and reading. When in doubt, check a 
dictionary rather than guessing.


RULE 3: THE ORDER OF MULTIPLE ADJECTIVES
----------------------------------------------
When you stack several adjectives before one noun, they follow a 
special order, like guests lining up for a photo:

  SIZE/SHAPE  -->  AGE  -->  COLOR  -->  ORIGIN  -->  MATERIAL  -->  NOUN

Examples:
  A tiny round antique gold locket
  A large new red French sports car
  A soft old white cotton blanket
  Spicy Mexican street tacos

Memory trick: Think "SIZE before AGE before COLOR before ORIGIN before 
MATERIAL" — like unpacking a suitcase from the outside in: first how 
big it is, then how old, then what color, then where it's from, then 
what it's made of.


RULE 4: "THE + ADJECTIVE" CAN NAME A GROUP OF PEOPLE
----------------------------------------------------------
When you place "the" in front of certain adjectives, it turns the 
adjective into a noun that represents a whole group of people.

  the young       = young people in general
  the elderly     = elderly people in general
  the wealthy     = wealthy people in general
  the unemployed  = unemployed people in general
  the injured     = injured people in general

Example sentence:
  This new park was designed especially for the elderly.
  The charity raises money to support the homeless.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try building your own adjective "sandwich" using the order above. 
Start with just one adjective, then slowly add more:

  A car.
  A red car.
  A small red car.
  A small new red car.
  A small new red Italian car.

Say each sentence out loud and notice how naturally the words fall 
into place once you know the order.
