===================================
COORDINATING AND CORRELATIVE CONJUNCTIONS
===================================

WHAT IS A CONJUNCTION?
---------------------------
A conjunction is a connector word — it joins words, phrases, or whole 
clauses together in a sentence. Think of conjunctions as the "glue" 
that holds sentence parts together.

  I bought apples and bananas.
  Since it's snowing, school is closed today.
  He didn't call, but he texted instead.


THE THREE TYPES OF CONJUNCTIONS
--------------------------------------
There are three main families of conjunctions:

  1. COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS
     Connect two equal, independent ideas.
     and, but, or, so, for, yet, nor

  2. CORRELATIVE CONJUNCTIONS
     Come in matching pairs.
     both/and, either/or, neither/nor, not only/but also

  3. SUBORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS
     Start a dependent clause (a clause that can't stand alone).
     although, after, before, because, if, since, unless, when, 
     while, until, so that, whether, where

Today's focus is on the first two types: COORDINATING and 
CORRELATIVE conjunctions.


PART 1: COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS — CONNECTING EQUAL IDEAS
===================================
These seven conjunctions are often remembered using the acronym 
FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So. Each one connects two 
equal, independent pieces of a sentence — think of them as joining 
two puzzle pieces that are the same size and shape.


AND — MEANS "IN ADDITION TO"
------------------------------------
  We visited a museum and a botanical garden on our trip.
  She plays the guitar and the violin.


BUT — SHOWS CONTRAST OR DISAGREEMENT
--------------------------------------------
  I love spicy food, but my sister can't handle it at all.
  He is quiet in class, but he's very talkative with friends.


OR — SHOWS A CHOICE BETWEEN OPTIONS
--------------------------------------------
  Would you like tea or coffee?
  We can watch a comedy or a documentary tonight.


SO — SHOWS A RESULT
---------------------------
  The restaurant had great reviews, so we decided to try it.
  It started pouring rain, so we canceled the picnic.


FOR — MEANS "BECAUSE" (MORE FORMAL/LITERARY)
--------------------------------------------------
  I decided to stay home, for I was feeling under the weather.
  She trusted him completely, for he had never lied to her.


YET — SHOWS UNEXPECTED CONTRAST
--------------------------------------
  The exam was extremely difficult, yet she passed with ease.
  He trains every single day, yet he rarely wins competitions.


NOR — CONNECTS TWO NEGATIVE IDEAS
-----------------------------------------
  She doesn't like coffee, nor does she enjoy tea.
  He didn't apologize, nor did he explain himself.

Memory trick: Picture FANBOYS as seven bridges — each one connects 
two equally sized islands (independent ideas) sitting side by side.


PART 2: CORRELATIVE CONJUNCTIONS — WORDS THAT WORK IN PAIRS
===================================
Correlative conjunctions always travel in twos, like a matched set of 
socks — you can't use one without the other.


BOTH...AND
-------------------
Used to emphasize two things together equally.

  She excelled in both academics and sports.
  Both my brother and my sister live abroad now.


EITHER...OR
------------------
Used to present two possible choices.

  You can choose either the window seat or the aisle seat.
  I'm fine with either pizza or tacos for dinner.


NEITHER...NOR
--------------------
Used to reject two options at the same time (a double negative pair).

  He enjoys neither hiking nor camping.
  Neither my parents nor my siblings knew about the surprise party.


NOT ONLY...BUT ALSO
--------------------------
Used to add emphasis by highlighting two things, showing that the 
second is just as notable (or more) than the first.

  Not only blue but also yellow suits your skin tone.
  She earned top marks in not only chemistry but also physics.

Memory trick: Correlative conjunctions are like a matching outfit — 
if you wear one half, you must wear the other half too.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try combining two of your own sentences using a coordinating 
conjunction, then rewrite the idea using a correlative pair:

  Coordinating: I like hiking, and I like swimming.
  Correlative: I like both hiking and swimming.

  Coordinating: You can call me, or you can text me.
  Correlative: You can either call me or text me.

Practicing both styles side by side helps you see how flexible 
English sentence-building can be.
