===================================
PREPOSITIONS: "WITH," "OVER," AND "BY"
===================================

A QUICK REMINDER ABOUT PREPOSITIONS
------------------------------------------
These small connector words show relationships between people, 
objects, actions, and ideas. As always, the best way to master them 
is to notice how they're used in real sentences and practice using 
them yourself.


THE BIG PICTURE: THREE DIFFERENT FEELINGS
--------------------------------------------------
  WITH  = togetherness (being alongside, having, or using something)
  OVER  = movement across or beyond (crossing, exceeding, spanning)
  BY    = closeness or method (near something, or the way it's done)


PART 1: "WITH" — TOGETHERNESS, POSSESSION, TOOLS, AND FEELINGS
===================================

USE A: BEING TOGETHER OR INVOLVED
----------------------------------------
  I ordered a burger with a side salad.
  She was with her roommate when the fire alarm went off.
  He has been volunteering with the local shelter.
  The doctor will be with you in a few minutes.

USE B: SHOWING "HAVING" SOMETHING
------------------------------------------
  I spoke to a woman with curly red hair.
  Were you the person with the loud laugh?
  People with busy schedules often skip breakfast.

USE C: SHOWING "USING" SOMETHING (A TOOL OR METHOD)
------------------------------------------------------------
  I fixed the chair with a screwdriver.
  This soup is made with garlic and ginger.
  She trimmed the hedge with electric shears.

USE D: SHOWING FEELING OR MANNER
--------------------------------------------
  He accepted the award with gratitude.
  She walked onto the stage with confidence.

USE E: SHOWING AGREEMENT OR UNDERSTANDING
--------------------------------------------------
  Are you with me on this plan?
  Yes, I'm totally with you.
  He agrees with the new policy.

Memory trick: "With" is the "side-by-side" word — picture two things 
standing shoulder to shoulder, connected somehow.


PART 2: "OVER" — MOVEMENT, CROSSING, AND EXCEEDING
===================================

USE A: MOVEMENT FROM ONE PLACE TO ANOTHER
--------------------------------------------------
  Come over to our place for the game night.
  Could you scoot over a little?
  They sent over some snacks for the office party.

USE B: MOVEMENT DOWNWARD OR ACROSS
------------------------------------------
  The ladder tipped over in the wind.
  Can you lean over and grab my keys?
  He knocked the vase over by accident.

USE C: MORE THAN AN EXPECTED AMOUNT
------------------------------------------
  This bill is over our monthly budget.
  Guests fifteen and over need a separate ticket.
  The call lasted for over an hour.

USE D: A SPAN OF TIME
------------------------------
  I lived in that apartment over three years.
  She hasn't visited over the past few months.

Memory trick: "Over" always suggests crossing something — a 
distance, a surface, a limit, or a stretch of time.


PART 3: "BY" — NEARNESS, PASSIVE DOERS, AND METHODS
===================================

USE A: SHOWING NEARNESS OR PROXIMITY
------------------------------------------
  Can I stand by the window?
  He was waiting by the entrance.
  The bakery is by the train station.

USE B: SHOWING WHO DID THE ACTION (IN PASSIVE VOICE)
------------------------------------------------------------
  The engine was repaired by a technician.
  The package was delivered by a courier.
  The old warehouse was demolished by the construction crew.

USE C: SHOWING AN ACTION WITH A SPECIFIC PURPOSE
------------------------------------------------------------
  You can improve your health by exercising regularly.
  I showed my appreciation by sending a thank-you card.
  She achieved her goal by staying consistent.

USE D: SHOWING A METHOD OR MEANS
------------------------------------------
  Please ship this order by express delivery.
  I traveled to the conference by train.

Memory trick: "By" often points to either WHO did something (in 
passive voice) or HOW something got done — think of it as the 
"nearby or by-what-means" word.


QUICK REFERENCE CHART
------------------------------------------------------------
 PREPOSITION | USED FOR                          | EXAMPLE
------------------------------------------------------------
 With        | Togetherness, having, using, feeling | coffee with milk
 Over        | Movement, crossing, exceeding, time    | over an hour
 By          | Nearness, passive doer, method          | by train
------------------------------------------------------------


PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try building one sentence with each preposition about your own day:

  WITH: I had breakfast with ____.
  OVER: I spent over ____ minutes doing ____.
  BY: I traveled to ____ by ____.

The more sentences you practice, the more natural these connector 
words will start to feel.
