WHAT IS AN ACTION VERB?
--------------------------
An action verb shows something a person, animal, or thing DOES. 
These are the most common verbs in English — we use them all day long!

  I cook dinner.
  You dance beautifully.
  The birds sing at sunrise.
  My car breaks down often.
  We laugh at his jokes.


RULE 1: ADD "S" FOR HE / SHE / IT
------------------------------------
Think of it like a little bonus letter. Whenever the subject is ONE 
person or thing (he, she, it — or a singular name), add "s" to the 
end of the verb.

  I walk to school.        -->   He walks to school.
  You drink coffee.        -->   She drinks coffee.
  We jump rope.            -->   It jumps over the fence.
  They swim every day.     -->   My cat swims in the pool.

Memory trick: "One person, one extra letter." 
  He, She, It --> add "s"
  I, You, We, They --> no "s"


RULE 2: MAKING NEGATIVE SENTENCES
-------------------------------------
To make an action verb negative, we borrow a helper word: 
DO, DOES, or DID. Then we add "not."

  Use "do not" with I, you, we, they:
    I do not drink coffee.
    You do not drink coffee.
    We do not drink coffee.
    They do not drink coffee.

  Use "does not" with he, she, it:
    He does not drink coffee.
    She does not drink coffee.
    It does not bark at night.

  Use "did not" for the past, with ANY subject:
    I did not finish my homework.
    She did not call me back.
    They did not arrive on time.

Important: once you use "do/does/did," the main verb goes back to its 
plain form — no "s," no "-ed."
  Wrong: He does not eats bread.
  Right: He does not eat bread.


RULE 3: ASKING QUESTIONS
----------------------------
To ask a question with an action verb, put DO, DOES, or DID at the 
very beginning of the sentence.

  Do you drink coffee?
  Does he drink coffee?
  Does she call her mom every week?
  Did they arrive on time?
  Did you finish your homework?


RULE 4: SHORTENING WITH CONTRACTIONS
----------------------------------------
In casual, everyday English, we shrink these helper words down:

  do not   -->  don't
  does not -->  doesn't
  did not  -->  didn't

Examples:
  I don't drink coffee.
  She doesn't call her mom every week.
  It doesn't bark at night.
  They didn't arrive on time.


QUICK REFERENCE CHART
------------------------------------------------------------------
 SUBJECT        | POSITIVE       | NEGATIVE            | QUESTION
------------------------------------------------------------------
 I              | I dance.       | I don't dance.      | Do I dance?
 You            | You dance.     | You don't dance.    | Do you dance?
 He / She       | He dances.     | He doesn't dance.   | Does he dance?
 It             | It dances.     | It doesn't dance.   | Does it dance?
 We             | We dance.      | We don't dance.     | Do we dance?
 They           | They danced.   | They didn't dance.  | Did they dance?
------------------------------------------------------------------


PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Think of DO, DOES, and DID as helper robots. They stand in front of 
the main verb to ask questions or say "no" — and once a helper robot 
shows up, the main verb relaxes back to its simplest form.

Try describing your daily routine using these patterns:
  I wake up early.
  My brother doesn't wake up early.
  Does your sister wake up early?
  We didn't sleep well last night.
