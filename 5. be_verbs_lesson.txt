WHAT IS A VERB?
----------------
A verb tells us an ACTION (something you do) or a STATE (something you are).

Look at these action sentences:
  She runs every morning.
  The children play in the park.
  We eat dinner together.
  My dog barks at strangers.
  He reads a new book every week.

Now look at these "state of being" sentences. Nothing is happening — 
they just describe how someone or something EXISTS:
  She is happy.
  The soup is hot.
  They are my neighbors.

These small but powerful words — am, is, are — are called "BE" VERBS.
They connect the subject to a description, a place, or an identity.


RULE 1: THE VERB MUST MATCH THE SUBJECT
-----------------------------------------
Just like shoes come in different sizes, "be" verbs come in different 
forms to match different subjects.

  I am a teacher.
  You are my best friend.
  He is a football player.
  She is from Spain.
  It is a sunny day.
  We are classmates.
  They are on vacation.


RULE 2: MAKING NEGATIVE SENTENCES
-----------------------------------
To make a sentence negative, simply add "not" right after the "be" verb.

  I am not a teacher.
  You are not my enemy.
  He is not a football player.
  She is not from France.
  It is not raining.
  We are not late.
  They are not on vacation.


RULE 3: ASKING QUESTIONS
--------------------------
To ask a question, just move the "be" verb to the very front of the 
sentence — before the subject.

  Am I late?
  Are you my best friend?
  Is he a football player?
  Is she from Spain?
  Is it raining?
  Are we classmates?
  Are they on vacation?


RULE 4: SHORTENING WITH CONTRACTIONS
---------------------------------------
In everyday, casual English, we often shrink "is not" and "are not" 
into one word. This is called a CONTRACTION.

  is not   -->  isn't
  are not  -->  aren't

Examples:
  He isn't a football player.
  She isn't from Spain.
  It isn't raining.
  We aren't classmates.
  They aren't on vacation.

Note: "am not" has no short contraction in statements, but in questions 
we often say "Aren't I...?" instead of "Am I not...?"
  Example: Aren't I invited to the party?

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
----------------------------
Think of "be" verbs like a mirror — they simply reflect what the 
subject already is. There's no action, just identity or description.

Try this memory trick:
  I  --> am    (only "I" uses "am")
  He / She / It  --> is   (one person or thing = "is")
  You / We / They  --> are   (more than one, or "you" = "are")

Practice by describing yourself, your family, and your classroom:
  I am a student.
  My brother is tall.
  My friends are funny.
  The classroom is not messy.
  Is your teacher strict?
