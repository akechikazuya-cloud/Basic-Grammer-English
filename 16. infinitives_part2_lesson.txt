THE "IT IS + INFINITIVE" PATTERN
--------------------------------------
One very common way to use an infinitive is with the pattern:

  IT + BE VERB + (ADJECTIVE/NOUN) + INFINITIVE

Here, "it" acts like a placeholder that points forward to the real 
subject — the infinitive phrase later in the sentence.

  It is time to leave.
  It is unusual to see snow in April.
  It is wise to save some money each month.
  It was fun to catch up with old friends.
  It was a privilege to work with such a talented team.
  It was an honor to receive that award.
  It is exciting to start a new job.
  It was amazing to watch the fireworks together.

Memory trick: Think of "it" as a placeholder sign that says "the real 
subject is coming — just wait for it!" The infinitive at the end is 
the true star of the sentence.


GERUNDS VS. INFINITIVES AFTER VERBS
-----------------------------------------
Both gerunds ("-ing") and infinitives ("to + verb") can act like 
nouns, but here's the catch: certain verbs only allow ONE of the two 
to follow them. Let's break this into four easy-to-remember groups.


GROUP 1: VERBS FOLLOWED ONLY BY A GERUND
-----------------------------------------------
These verbs are always followed by the "-ing" form, never "to + verb."

  consider, suggest, enjoy, deny, avoid, miss, mind, practice, 
  postpone, resist, finish, quit, give up, put off

Examples:
  I enjoy hiking on the weekends.
  She suggested going to the new restaurant.
  He finally quit smoking last year.
  Would you mind closing the window?
  They postponed traveling until next month.

Memory trick: Picture these verbs as strict teachers who only accept 
homework in "-ing" format — no exceptions!


GROUP 2: VERBS FOLLOWED ONLY BY AN INFINITIVE
------------------------------------------------------
These verbs are always followed by "to + verb," never the "-ing" 
form.

  offer, decide, hope, attempt, promise, agree, afford, deserve, 
  refuse, undertake, learn, fail, seem, appear, tend, pretend, 
  choose, demand, desire, guarantee, claim, manage, determine, 
  expect, want, wish

Examples:
  She decided to move to a new city.
  He promised to call me back.
  We can't afford to buy a new car right now.
  They refused to give up.
  I hope to visit Japan someday.

Memory trick: These verbs are the opposite strict teachers — they 
only accept homework with "to" attached, every single time.


GROUP 3: VERBS FOLLOWED BY EITHER, WITH NO CHANGE IN MEANING
--------------------------------------------------------------------
These flexible verbs are fine with either a gerund or an infinitive — 
and the meaning stays exactly the same either way.

  continue, like, love, begin, start, propose, neglect, stand, hate

Examples:
  It started raining. = It started to rain.
  She continued working. = She continued to work.
  I love reading. = I love to read.
  He began cooking. = He began to cook.

Memory trick: These verbs are the "easygoing" teachers — they don't 
care which format you use, as long as the work gets done.


GROUP 4: VERBS FOLLOWED BY EITHER, BUT THE MEANING CHANGES
------------------------------------------------------------------
A small handful of verbs are trickier — using a gerund versus an 
infinitive after them completely changes the meaning of the sentence.

  forget, remember, stop, try

Examples:
  STOP:
    I stopped texting while driving. 
    (= I no longer text while driving; the habit ended.)
    I stopped to text my friend. 
    (= I paused what I was doing in order to text.)

  REMEMBER:
    I remember locking the door. 
    (= I have a memory of the action already happening.)
    I remembered to lock the door. 
    (= I didn't forget to do it; a reminder to complete a task.)

  TRY:
    I tried exercising every morning. 
    (= I experimented with a method to see if it worked.)
    I tried to exercise every morning. 
    (= I made an effort or attempt, though I may not have succeeded.)

Memory trick: With this group, always double-check the meaning you 
intend — gerund and infinitive versions tell two different stories!

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
When you learn a new verb, ask yourself: "Does this verb prefer 
gerunds, infinitives, both, or does it change meaning?" Keeping a 
small notebook divided into these four groups can help you build 
strong instincts over time.
