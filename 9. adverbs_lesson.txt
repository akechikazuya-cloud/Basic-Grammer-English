WHAT IS AN ADVERB?
----------------------
An adverb adds extra detail to a VERB, an ADJECTIVE, or even ANOTHER 
ADVERB. Think of an adverb as a helper that answers questions like: 
How? How much? To what degree?

Adverbs are like seasoning in cooking — the main dish (the verb or 
adjective) is already there, but the adverb adds flavor and detail.


RULE 1: ADVERBS CAN DESCRIBE A VERB
-----------------------------------------
An adverb often tells us HOW an action happens.

  The cat moves silently across the room.
  Please explain the rules clearly.
  I check my email constantly.
  The rain fell steadily all afternoon.

Notice how each adverb answers the question "how?"
  moves HOW? --> silently
  explain HOW? --> clearly
  check HOW OFTEN? --> constantly
  fell HOW? --> steadily


RULE 2: ADVERBS CAN DESCRIBE AN ADJECTIVE
-----------------------------------------------
An adverb can also boost or soften an adjective, telling us TO WHAT 
DEGREE something is true.

  This puzzle is incredibly difficult!
  The soup is slightly spicy, so taste it first.
  Her performance was absolutely fantastic!
  That movie was fairly boring.

Here, the adverb sits right before the adjective and tells us "how 
difficult," "how spicy," "how fantastic," or "how boring."


RULE 3: ADVERBS CAN DESCRIBE ANOTHER ADVERB
--------------------------------------------------
Sometimes an adverb describes another adverb, adding even more detail 
about degree or intensity.

  She sings extremely well.
  Traffic moves much slower during rush hour.
  I visit my grandparents far less often than before.

In these examples, one adverb (extremely, much, far) is boosting 
another adverb (well, slower, often).


RULE 4: TURNING ADJECTIVES INTO ADVERBS WITH "-LY"
--------------------------------------------------------
A simple way to build an adverb is to take an adjective you already 
know and add "-ly" to the end.

  quiet    --> quietly
  soft     --> softly
  careful  --> carefully
  bright   --> brightly
  honest   --> honestly
  perfect  --> perfectly

Memory trick: "-ly" is like a costume the adjective puts on to become 
an adverb — same word, new job!


RULE 5: ADJECTIVES ENDING IN "-Y" --> CHANGE TO "-ILY"
--------------------------------------------------------------
If the adjective ends in "y," swap the "y" for an "i" before adding 
"-ly."

  angry   --> angrily
  hungry  --> hungrily
  lazy    --> lazily
  noisy   --> noisily
  greedy  --> greedily

Memory trick: Same rule as before — "y" changes to "i" so it can 
comfortably wear its "-ly" costume.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Pick any action you did today and describe HOW you did it, using an 
adverb:

  I ate breakfast quickly.
  I answered the phone calmly.
  I finished my homework easily.

Then try boosting an adjective with an adverb:

  The weather is unusually warm today.
  This book is incredibly interesting.
