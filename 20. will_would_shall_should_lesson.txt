===================================
AUXILIARY VERBS: "WILL/WOULD" AND "SHALL/SHOULD"
===================================

MODAL AUXILIARY VERBS: HELPERS THAT NEVER STAND ALONE
------------------------------------------------------------------
Words like will, would, shall, should, can, could, may, might, and 
must are called MODAL AUXILIARY VERBS. Unlike "be," "do," or "have," 
these words can NEVER work alone as a main verb — they always need a 
main verb attached right after them.

  Correct:   I will help you.
  Incorrect: I will you. (missing the main verb!)

Think of modal verbs as a car that always needs a driver (the main 
verb) — the car itself can't go anywhere without one.


PART 1: "WILL" — TALKING ABOUT THE FUTURE AND MORE
===================================

USE A: EXPRESSING WILLINGNESS OR CONSENT
------------------------------------------------
  I will take care of the paperwork.
  Will you please lower your voice?
  He will lend you his car if you ask nicely.


USE B: PREDICTING THE FUTURE
----------------------------------
  It will snow later this evening.
  The results will arrive next week.
  Prices will likely increase next year.


USE C: DESCRIBING CAPACITY OR ABILITY
-------------------------------------------
  This tank will hold fifty gallons of water.
  This stadium will seat 60,000 fans.
  This suitcase will fit all my clothes for the trip.


USE D: SHOWING DETERMINATION
----------------------------------
  I will finish this marathon no matter what.
  She will succeed, even if it takes years.

Memory trick: "Will" is like a promise flag waving into the future — 
it points forward to something not yet done.


PART 2: "WOULD" — THE PAST FORM OF "WILL," WITH MANY JOBS
===================================

USE A: EXPRESSING PREFERENCE (OFTEN WITH "RATHER")
--------------------------------------------------------
  I would rather stay home tonight.
  We'd rather walk than take the bus.


USE B: EXPRESSING A WISH OR DESIRE
----------------------------------------
  I would love to visit Japan someday.
  She would like some more tea, please.


USE C: SHOWING POSSIBILITY OR IMAGINED SITUATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------
  If I were the manager, I would change the schedule.
  If it rained, we would cancel the picnic.


USE D: DESCRIBING PAST HABITS OR ROUTINES
------------------------------------------------
  When I was a kid, we would visit my grandmother every summer.
  Back then, he would jog every morning before work.

Memory trick: Think of "would" as "will" wearing a "past tense 
costume" — same flexible spirit, just shifted into memory, wishes, or 
imagination.


PART 3: "SHALL" — FORMAL AND POLITE USES
===================================

USE A: MAKING POLITE SUGGESTIONS OR OFFERS (COMMON IN QUESTIONS)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Shall we get started?
  Shall I open a window?
  Let's take a break, shall we?

Note: In everyday American English, "will" is used far more often 
than "shall" to talk about the future. "Shall" mostly survives in 
polite questions and formal writing.


USE B: EXPRESSING FORMAL OBLIGATION OR RULES
--------------------------------------------------
  All employees shall follow the safety guidelines.
  There shall be no smoking inside the building.
  Visitors shall register at the front desk.

Memory trick: "Shall" in rules and contracts sounds like a stamped, 
official order — very formal and firm.


PART 4: "SHOULD" — THE PAST FORM OF "SHALL," USED FOR ADVICE
===================================

USE A: GIVING OPINIONS, SUGGESTIONS, OR ADVICE
--------------------------------------------------
  You should drink more water every day.
  I should probably leave earlier tomorrow.
  He should ask for help if he's struggling.


USE B: EXPRESSING REGRET ("SHOULD HAVE + PAST PARTICIPLE")
------------------------------------------------------------------
This form shows something that was supposed to happen but didn't.

  You should have tried the dessert. It was incredible!
  I should have studied more before the exam.
  We should have left earlier to avoid the traffic.

Memory trick: "Should have" is like looking back at a fork in the 
road you didn't take — expressing regret over a missed opportunity.


USE C: ASKING FOR SOMEONE'S OPINION
-----------------------------------------
  What should we order for dinner?
  Should we reschedule the meeting?
  Where should we go for vacation this year?


USE D: DESCRIBING WHAT IS EXPECTED OR LIKELY
--------------------------------------------------
  The package should arrive by Friday.
  Everyone should be here by 6 PM.
  The results should be ready soon.


QUICK REFERENCE CHART
------------------------------------------------------------
 MODAL VERB | COMMON USES                        | EXAMPLE
------------------------------------------------------------
 Will       | Future, willingness, ability        | It will rain.
 Would      | Preference, wish, past habit         | I would rather stay.
 Shall      | Polite suggestion, formal rule        | Shall we go?
 Should     | Advice, regret, expectation            | You should rest.
------------------------------------------------------------


PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Practice building one sentence for each modal verb about your own 
life:

  WILL: I will ____ tomorrow.
  WOULD: I would rather ____ than ____.
  SHALL: Shall we ____?
  SHOULD: I should ____ more often.

The more you use these modal verbs in real sentences, the more 
naturally their different "flavors" of meaning will click into 
place.
