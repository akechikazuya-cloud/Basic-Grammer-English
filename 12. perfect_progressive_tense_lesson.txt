WHAT IS THE PERFECT PROGRESSIVE TENSE?
--------------------------------------------
The perfect progressive tense is a combo tense — it mixes the 
"connecting bridge" feeling of the perfect tense with the "action in 
motion" feeling of the progressive tense. It describes something that 
happened continuously, over a stretch of time, and links it to 
another moment: now, a moment in the past, or a moment in the future.

Think of it like a movie clip that plays for a while and then 
connects to a specific scene — showing not just that something 
happened, but that it kept happening, right up until that moment.


RULE 1: PRESENT PERFECT PROGRESSIVE
-----------------------------------------
FORMULA: have/has + been + verb-ing

This shows a continuous action that started in the past and either 
just stopped or is still connected to right now — usually explaining 
a current result or condition.

  I have been cooking all morning, and the kitchen still smells amazing.
  He has been jogging, and now he needs some water.
  They have been painting the fence, and their hands are covered in paint.
  We have been waiting for an hour, and we are getting impatient.

Memory trick: Something was happening for a while, and you can still 
"feel" its effect right now.


RULE 2: PAST PERFECT PROGRESSIVE
--------------------------------------
FORMULA: had + been + verb-ing

This shows a continuous action in the past that was already going on 
BEFORE another past event happened. It sets the scene for something 
else that occurred.

  I had been cooking all morning, and the kitchen still smelled amazing 
  when the guests arrived.
  He had been jogging, and he still needed water when he got home.
  They had been painting the fence, and their hands were still covered 
  in paint when the rain started.
  We had been waiting for an hour when the bus finally showed up.

Memory trick: This is the "meanwhile, back in the past" tense — one 
long action was already unfolding when something else interrupted it.


RULE 3: FUTURE PERFECT PROGRESSIVE
----------------------------------------
FORMULA: will + have + been + verb-ing

This shows a continuous action that will keep going up until a 
specific point in the future — highlighting the total duration.

  By tonight, I will have been cooking for six hours straight.
  By next month, he will have been jogging every day for a whole year.
  By the time the guests arrive, they will have been painting the 
  fence since early morning.
  By 6 PM, we will have been waiting for three hours.

Memory trick: Imagine a stopwatch that keeps running until a future 
deadline — this tense highlights just how long the action will have 
lasted by that point.


THREE VERBS IN ALL THREE PERFECT PROGRESSIVE TENSES
-----------------------------------------------------------
COOK
  I have been cooking, and the kitchen still smells wonderful.
  I had been cooking, and the kitchen still smelled wonderful when 
  they arrived.
  By tonight, I will have been cooking for six hours straight.

JOG
  She has been jogging, and she is now completely out of breath.
  She had been jogging, and she was still out of breath when she got 
  home.
  By next summer, she will have been jogging for almost a year.

PRACTICE (PIANO)
  He has been practicing the piano, and he plays much better now.
  He had been practicing the piano, and he had already gotten much 
  better by the recital.
  By the time of the concert, he will have been practicing the piano 
  for several months.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try filling in this pattern with your own actions:

  I have been ____ing, and now I feel ____.
  I had been ____ing, and I still felt ____ when ____ happened.
  By ____, I will have been ____ing for ____.

Example:
  I have been studying, and now I feel tired.
  I had been studying, and I still felt tired when my friend called.
  By midnight, I will have been studying for five hours.
