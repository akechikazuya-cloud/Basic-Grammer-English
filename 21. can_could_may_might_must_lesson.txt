===================================
AUXILIARY VERBS: "CAN/COULD" AND "MAY/MIGHT/MUST"
===================================

MODAL VERBS FOR ABILITY, PERMISSION, POSSIBILITY, AND NECESSITY
------------------------------------------------------------------------
This group of modal verbs helps us talk about what someone IS ABLE 
to do, what someone is ALLOWED to do, what MIGHT happen, and what 
someone MUST do. Like all modal verbs, they always need a main verb 
right after them.


PART 1: "CAN" — ABILITY, PERMISSION, AND REQUESTS
===================================

USE A: EXPRESSING ABILITY
--------------------------------
  I can bake a three-layer cake from scratch.
  She can't ride a bicycle yet.
  Can you lift this box by yourself?

USE B: ASKING FOR PERMISSION
------------------------------------
  Can I borrow your charger?
  Can I take a short break?
  Can I turn down the air conditioning?

USE C: MAKING REQUESTS OR SUGGESTIONS
--------------------------------------------
  Can I get a refill, please?
  Can I see the menu again?
  You can sit here if you want.
  You can choose whichever color you like.

Memory trick: Think of "can" as your everyday, casual toolbox word — 
useful for skills, quick permission, and simple requests.


PART 2: "COULD" — THE PAST FORM OF "CAN," WITH EXTRA POLITENESS
===================================

USE A: DESCRIBING A PAST ABILITY
--------------------------------------
  I could climb trees easily when I was a child.
  You could hear the waves crashing from our balcony.
  They could tell something was wrong.

USE B: ASKING FOR PERMISSION MORE POLITELY
--------------------------------------------------
  Could I borrow your pen for a moment?
  You could take my seat if you'd like.
  Could you please hold the elevator?
  Could I get another napkin?

USE C: EXPRESSING POSSIBILITY
------------------------------------
  All of us could fit in one car.
  You could always call me if you need help.
  Could this rumor be true?
  This idea could actually succeed.

Memory trick: "Could" is "can" dressed up in its "polite outfit" — 
same meaning, just softer and more formal-sounding.


PART 3: "MAY" — FORMAL PERMISSION AND POLITE POSSIBILITY
===================================

USE A: ASKING FOR FORMAL PERMISSION
--------------------------------------------
  May I enter the room?
  May I add one more comment?
  May I ask a quick question?

USE B: SUGGESTING SOMETHING IS POSSIBLE
------------------------------------------------
  He may accept the job offer.
  They may not be aware of the schedule change.
  It may snow later tonight.

Memory trick: "May" is the most formal member of this group — 
picture it being used in a job interview or a courtroom.


PART 4: "MIGHT" — THE PAST FORM OF "MAY," SHOWING SMALLER POSSIBILITY
===================================

"Might" suggests a smaller, more uncertain chance than "may" — and in 
everyday American English, "might" is actually used far more often 
than "may."

  She might have already left for the airport.
  I might take a nap this afternoon.
  He might not show up to the party.
  This answer might be correct.
  You might have dropped your wallet.
  The shop might have closed early today.

Memory trick: If "may" is a 50/50 guess, "might" often feels more 
like a "maybe, but I'm really not sure" guess.


PART 5: "MUST" — STRONG NECESSITY OR STRONG CERTAINTY
===================================

USE A: EXPRESSING SOMETHING REQUIRED OR NECESSARY
--------------------------------------------------------
  I must submit this application by Friday.
  Every driver must wear a seatbelt.
  Citizens must pay their taxes on time.
  This building must have an emergency exit.
  You must answer this question honestly.

USE B: EXPRESSING STRONG CERTAINTY OR A LOGICAL CONCLUSION
------------------------------------------------------------------
  She must be exhausted after that long flight.
  You must be kidding!
  There must be some kind of misunderstanding.
  He must be the new manager everyone mentioned.

Memory trick: "Must" carries a heavy, no-wiggle-room feeling — either 
it's a strict rule (necessity), or you're almost 100% sure about 
something (certainty).


QUICK REFERENCE CHART
------------------------------------------------------------
 MODAL VERB | COMMON USES                       | EXAMPLE
------------------------------------------------------------
 Can        | Ability, casual permission/request | I can swim.

 Might      | Smaller/uncertain possibility         | It might rain.
 Must       | Necessity, strong certainty           | You must rest.
------------------------------------------------------------


PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try building one sentence with each modal verb about your own day:

  CAN: I can ____.
  COULD: When I was younger, I could ____.
  MAY: May I ____?
  MIGHT: I might ____ later.
  MUST: I must ____ before tonight.

Practicing all five side by side helps you feel the difference in 
formality and certainty between each one.
