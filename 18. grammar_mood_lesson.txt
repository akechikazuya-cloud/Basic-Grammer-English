===================================
INDICATIVE, IMPERATIVE, SUBJUNCTIVE MOOD
===================================

WHAT IS "MOOD" IN GRAMMAR?
--------------------------------
Mood shows the speaker's or writer's ATTITUDE toward what they are 
saying. Are they stating a fact? Giving a command? Imagining 
something that isn't true? English has three main moods, and each 
one has its own "flavor."


MOOD 1: INDICATIVE — STATING FACTS OR REAL SITUATIONS
------------------------------------------------------------
The indicative mood is the most common mood. It simply states facts, 
opinions, or real situations — things that ARE happening, DID happen, 
or WILL happen.

  We are meeting for lunch tomorrow.
  She finished her project last night.
  I believe this plan will work.
  The train leaves at 9 AM.

Memory trick: Think of "indicative" as "indicating" reality — just 
reporting what is true or expected to happen.


MOOD 2: IMPERATIVE — GIVING COMMANDS OR MAKING REQUESTS
------------------------------------------------------------------
The imperative mood gives an order, instruction, or invitation. The 
subject "you" is usually left out because it's understood.

  Close the door, please.
  Let's grab coffee this afternoon!
  Please turn down the music.
  Don't forget your umbrella.
  Hurry up, or we'll be late!

Memory trick: Imperative sentences often feel like a "command 
button" — short, direct, and action-focused.


MOOD 3: SUBJUNCTIVE — IMAGINING SOMETHING UNREAL OR HYPOTHETICAL
------------------------------------------------------------------------
The subjunctive mood expresses a wish, suggestion, doubt, or a 
situation that is NOT actually true — often something imaginary or 
contrary to fact. It commonly appears after "if" or "wish."

  If I were rich, I would travel the world.
  I wish I were taller.
  If she were here, she would know what to do.
  I wish this project were finished already.

Memory trick: Think of subjunctive mood as "pretend mode" — you're 
imagining a fantasy version of reality, not describing what's 
actually true.


THE SPECIAL RULE: "WERE" INSTEAD OF "WAS"
-------------------------------------------------
In the subjunctive mood, the verb "to be" always becomes "were," no 
matter the subject — even with I, he, she, or it, which would 
normally take "was."

  INCORRECT: If I was you, I would apply for that job.
  CORRECT:   If I were you, I would apply for that job.

  INCORRECT: She wishes she was more confident.
  CORRECT:   She wishes she were more confident.

Memory trick: In "pretend mode," everyone shares the same magic word: 
"were" — it doesn't matter who the subject is.


THE SPECIAL RULE: DROPPING "-S" FOR THIRD-PERSON SUBJECTS
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Certain verbs — like recommend, suggest, insist, request, demand, and 
propose — often trigger the subjunctive mood in the clause that 
follows them. In that clause, the verb stays in its base form, even 
for he/she/it, dropping the usual "-s" ending.

  INCORRECT: The manager insists that everyone arrives on time.
  CORRECT:   The manager insists that everyone arrive on time.

  INCORRECT: We suggest that he brings his laptop.
  CORRECT:   We suggest that he bring his laptop.

  INCORRECT: They requested that she submits the form early.
  CORRECT:   They requested that she submit the form early.

Memory trick: These "trigger verbs" (insist, suggest, recommend, 
request, demand) flip the following verb into its plain, base form — 
no "-s," no exceptions.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Try building one sentence in each mood about the same topic — like a 
job interview:

  INDICATIVE:   I have a job interview tomorrow.
  IMPERATIVE:   Prepare your answers tonight!
  SUBJUNCTIVE:  If I were more confident, the interview would feel 
                easier.

Practicing all three moods with the same topic helps you feel the 
difference between fact, command, and imagination.
