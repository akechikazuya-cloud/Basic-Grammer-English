WHAT IS VERB TENSE?
-----------------------
Verb tense tells us WHEN something happens: in the present, the past, 
or the future. Think of tense as a timestamp attached to every action.

There are three main time zones — present, past, and future — and 
each one has four "modes": simple, progressive, perfect, and perfect 
progressive. Today we focus on the SIMPLE tense, the most basic and 
commonly used form.

           PRESENT           PAST              FUTURE
SIMPLE     write             wrote             will write


THE BIG PICTURE: THREE TIME ZONES
----------------------------------------
Picture a timeline with three zones:

  PAST  <-----------  NOW  ----------->  FUTURE
  (already happened)  (happening now / 
                        general truth)    (hasn't happened yet)

Simple tense is the most direct way to talk about each zone — no 
extra decoration, just the plain fact of an action.


RULE 1: SIMPLE PRESENT = THE ORIGINAL VERB FORM
------------------------------------------------------
The simple present uses the verb almost exactly as it appears in the 
dictionary (remember to add "s" for he/she/it).

  I write a letter every Sunday.
  She writes a letter every Sunday.
  We drink tea in the morning.
  He drinks tea in the morning.

Use simple present for habits, routines, and general facts — things 
that are true regularly, not just right now.


RULE 2: SIMPLE PAST HAS A FEW PATTERNS
-----------------------------------------
The simple past shows something that already finished. Many verbs 
simply add "-ed," but many common verbs change completely and must be 
memorized. These are called IRREGULAR VERBS.

  Regular pattern (add -ed):
    walk --> walked
    play --> played
    clean --> cleaned

  Irregular pattern (verb changes shape):
    write --> wrote
    drink --> drank
    go --> went
    buy --> bought

Memory trick: Regular verbs "follow the rules," but irregular verbs 
are like rebellious students who do their own thing — you just have 
to learn their names.


RULE 3: SIMPLE FUTURE = WILL + BASE VERB
-------------------------------------------
The simple future adds the helper word "will" (or sometimes "shall") 
in front of the plain, unchanged verb.

  I will write a letter tomorrow.
  She will write a letter next week.
  We will drink coffee after the meeting.

Memory trick: "Will" is like a promise flag — it announces that 
something is going to happen later.


SIX VERBS IN ALL THREE TENSES
------------------------------------
Let's look at how a variety of verbs shift across present, past, and 
future.

WRITE
  I write a letter every weekend.        (present)
  I wrote a letter last weekend.         (past)
  I will write a letter next weekend.    (future)

DRINK
  I drink coffee every morning.
  I drank coffee an hour ago.
  I will drink coffee in a few minutes.

GO
  I go to the gym twice a week.
  I went to the gym yesterday.
  I will go to the gym tomorrow.

BUY
  I buy groceries every Friday.
  I bought groceries this morning.
  I will buy groceries this weekend.

UNDERSTAND
  I understand the lesson.
  I understood the lesson the day before yesterday.
  I will understand the lesson by next class.

STUDY
  I study Spanish.
  I studied Spanish for the last two years.
  I will study Spanish again next semester.

CLEAN
  I clean my apartment every weekend.
  I cleaned the kitchen already.
  I will clean my desk tomorrow morning.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Pick one everyday action and say it in all three tenses, like a 
mini time-travel exercise:

  I cook dinner every night.       (present – a routine)
  I cooked dinner last night.      (past – already done)
  I will cook dinner tonight.      (future – not done yet)

Try this with three of your own daily actions to build the habit of 
switching tenses naturally.
