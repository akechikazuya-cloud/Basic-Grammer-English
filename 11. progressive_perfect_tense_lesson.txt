PART 1: PROGRESSIVE TENSE
===================================

WHAT IS THE PROGRESSIVE TENSE?
------------------------------------
The progressive tense shows an action that is IN PROGRESS — it's 
happening, was happening, or will be happening at a certain moment. 
Think of it as hitting "pause" on a video: the action is caught 
mid-motion, not finished.

The formula is always the same:
  "BE" VERB (am/is/are, was/were, will be) + VERB + "-ing"


RULE: THREE TIME WINDOWS FOR THE PROGRESSIVE
--------------------------------------------------
PRESENT PROGRESSIVE (happening right now):
  am/is/are + verb-ing

PAST PROGRESSIVE (was happening at a moment in the past):
  was/were + verb-ing

FUTURE PROGRESSIVE (will be happening at a moment in the future):
  will be + verb-ing


SWIM
  I am swimming in the lake right now.        (present progressive)
  I was swimming in the lake this time yesterday.   (past progressive)
  I will be swimming in the lake next Saturday afternoon.  (future progressive)

READ
  I am reading a mystery novel at the moment.
  I was reading my book when the power went out.
  I will be reading on the plane tomorrow.

STUDY
  I am studying for my exam right now.
  I was studying when my friend called me.
  I will be studying at this time tomorrow night.

PAINT
  I am painting the fence this afternoon.
  I was painting the wall when it started to rain.
  I will be painting the room by the time you arrive.

Memory trick: Picture a photo taken in the middle of an action — the 
person is caught "in the act," not before or after. That's exactly 
what progressive tense captures.


PART 2: PERFECT TENSE
===================================

WHAT IS THE PERFECT TENSE?
--------------------------------
The perfect tense connects two points in time — it links a past 
action to another moment (now, a past moment, or a future moment). 
Think of it as a bridge connecting "then" to "now" (or another 
reference point).


RULE 1: PRESENT PERFECT (PAST ACTION CONNECTED TO NOW)
--------------------------------------------------------------
Use "have/has" + the past participle. This shows something that 
started in the past and is still relevant or true right now.

  have/has + past participle

  SWIM: I have swum in three different oceans this year.
  READ: She has read that book twice already.
  LIVE: We have lived in this city for ten years.


RULE 2: PAST PERFECT (ONE PAST ACTION HAPPENED BEFORE ANOTHER)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Use "had" + the past participle. This shows something that was 
already completed before another point in the past.

  had + past participle

  SWIM: I had swum across the lake before the lifeguard arrived.
  READ: She had read the whole series before the movie came out.
  LIVE: We had lived in Paris before we moved to Rome.


RULE 3: FUTURE PERFECT (SOMETHING WILL BE FINISHED BEFORE A FUTURE MOMENT)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Use "will have" + the past participle. This shows something that 
will already be completed by a specific point in the future.

  will have + past participle

  SWIM: I will have swum a hundred laps by the end of this month.
  READ: She will have read the entire book by Friday.
  LIVE: We will have lived here for five years by next spring.


FOUR VERBS IN ALL THREE PERFECT TENSES
--------------------------------------------
SWIM
  I have swum in the ocean many times this summer.       (present perfect)
  I had swum in that lake long before it was closed.      (past perfect)
  I will have swum a full mile by the end of training.   (future perfect)

STUDY
  I have studied French for three years now.
  I had studied French before I moved to Canada.
  I will have studied French for five years by graduation.

KNOW
  I have known my best friend since kindergarten.
  I had known him for years before we became roommates.
  I will have known her for a decade next January.

BAKE
  I have baked cookies every weekend this month.
  I had baked the cake before the guests arrived.
  I will have baked three batches by the end of the day.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Progressive = "caught in the act" (a snapshot of action happening).
Perfect = "a bridge between two points in time" (connecting past 
action to now, to another past moment, or to a future moment).

Try describing your day using both:
  Progressive: I am writing this sentence right now.
  Perfect: I have written five sentences already today.
