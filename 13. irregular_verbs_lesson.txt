REGULAR VS. IRREGULAR VERBS
--------------------------------
Every verb needs a PAST form and a PAST PARTICIPLE form (the one we 
use with "have/has/had" and in passive sentences). Most English verbs 
follow a simple rule — but a stubborn group of common verbs refuses 
to follow it!


PART 1: REGULAR VERBS
===================================
Regular verbs are easy — just add "-ed" (or sometimes just "-d") to 
the base verb to form both the past and the past participle. Same 
ending, every time.

 BASE VERB   | PAST        | PAST PARTICIPLE
------------------------------------------------
 play        | played      | played
 walk        | walked      | walked
 clean       | cleaned     | cleaned
 jump        | jumped      | jumped
 laugh       | laughed     | laughed
 dance       | danced      | danced
 wash        | washed      | washed

Memory trick: Regular verbs are like well-behaved students — they 
always follow the same "-ed" rule, no surprises.


PART 2: IRREGULAR VERBS
===================================
Irregular verbs don't follow the "-ed" rule. Instead of memorizing 
them one by one with no strategy, it helps to sort them into THREE 
FAMILIES based on their pattern. This makes them much easier to 
remember!


FAMILY 1: "NO CHANGE AT ALL" (BASE = PAST = PAST PARTICIPLE)
------------------------------------------------------------------
These verbs stay exactly the same in all three forms — nothing to 
memorize beyond recognizing them!

 BASE VERB   | PAST    | PAST PARTICIPLE
--------------------------------------------
 cost        | cost    | cost
 put         | put     | put
 hurt        | hurt    | hurt
 let         | let     | let
 split       | split   | split
 quit        | quit    | quit

Memory trick: These verbs are "frozen" — they refuse to change no 
matter what tense you use.


FAMILY 2: "TWIN FORM" (PAST = PAST PARTICIPLE, BUT DIFFERENT FROM BASE)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
These verbs change once, but then the past and past participle look 
identical to each other.

 BASE VERB   | PAST      | PAST PARTICIPLE
----------------------------------------------
 sleep       | slept     | slept
 sell        | sold      | sold
 tell        | told      | told
 send        | sent      | sent
 build       | built     | built
 catch       | caught    | caught
 bring       | brought   | brought
 think       | thought   | thought
 leave       | left      | left
 lend        | lent      | lent

Memory trick: Think of these as "twins" — the past and past 
participle forms are identical, so once you learn one change, you 
already know both.


FAMILY 3: "THREE DIFFERENT FACES" (BASE, PAST, AND PAST PARTICIPLE ALL DIFFER)
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------
These are the trickiest verbs — all three forms look different, so 
each one needs its own attention.

 BASE VERB   | PAST      | PAST PARTICIPLE
----------------------------------------------
 ride        | rode      | ridden
 write       | wrote     | written
 break       | broke     | broken
 choose      | chose     | chosen
 freeze      | froze     | frozen
 rise        | rose      | risen
 wake        | woke      | woken
 tear        | tore      | torn
 wear        | wore      | worn
 fall        | fell      | fallen
 see         | saw       | seen

Memory trick: Picture these verbs wearing three different costumes — 
one for each tense. There's no shortcut here; these just take extra 
practice and repetition.

PRACTICE TIP FOR LEARNERS
-----------------------------
Instead of memorizing irregular verbs in one giant random list, sort 
new verbs you learn into the three families above. Grouping them by 
pattern makes them much easier to recall later.

Try it yourself: which family do these verbs belong to?
  shut, drive, buy

(Answer: shut = Family 1, drive = Family 3, buy = Family 2)
